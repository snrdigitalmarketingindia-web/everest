Borehole Drilling in South Africa — What You Need to Know

What is borehole drilling in South Africa?

Borehole drilling in South Africa is the process of drilling a narrow vertical shaft into the earth to access underground water-bearing aquifers. A licensed drilling contractor uses a truck-mounted rig to penetrate soft and hard rock formations, typically to depths of 40–250 metres depending on the region and geological conditions. Once water is reached, the shaft is lined with a casing, fitted with a submersible pump or hand pump, and connected to surface storage — delivering a reliable, independent private water supply for residential, agricultural, or industrial use.

How does borehole drilling work?

The borehole drilling process in South Africa follows four stages: (1) Geophysical Survey — electromagnetic and resistivity scanning maps subsurface water-bearing formations, pinpointing the optimal drill location and increasing success rates from ~60% to over 90%. (2) Drilling — a truck-mounted rig such as the TATA Prima 2528A penetrates the ground using rotary drill bits, reaching confirmed water depth. (3) Pump Installation — a submersible pump or hand pump is installed and pressure-tested. (4) Commissioning — an overhead tank is constructed if required, pipework connected, and the full system tested before handover.

Under South Africa's National Water Act (Act 36 of 1998), boreholes used for domestic or agricultural purposes must be registered with the Department of Water and Sanitation.
